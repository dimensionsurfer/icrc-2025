2025 Impact Report

Research, Resilience, and Global Convening

In 2025, the Blavatnik Interdisciplinary Cyber Research Center funded new science, advanced visible research, and convened the global cyber and AI community during one of the most turbulent years the region has faced in recent memory.

When the 12-Day War disrupted our June conference calendar, we did not cancel our flagship events. We moved Cyber Week, AI Week, and DefenseTech Week to December—and brought together 11,000+ participants from more than 100 countries before the year ended.

Why this year mattered

We kept the global conversation moving.

This year combined research output, direct funding for new scientific work, and a decisive public-facing response to disruption. We did not simply preserve momentum; we turned pressure into visible institutional action.

The Blavatnik Interdisciplinary Cyber Research Center continued to fund science, produce visible research outcomes, and host major international convenings. The turning point came in June. We moved our flagship conference platform to December, delivered the full sequence before the year ended, and reaffirmed our role as a place where research, policy, and industry still meet at scale.

About the center

Research, funding, outreach—built to move together.

Official center language emphasizes interdisciplinary scientific research on the information age, digital transformation, and cybersecurity, combined with a competitive research fund and outreach activity. The center also describes Cyber Week and AI Week as grand-scale annual international events that bring together experts, industry leaders, startups, investors, academics, diplomats, and government officials.

Visible research outcomes

Three projects capture how the Blavatnik Interdisciplinary Cyber Research Center turned academic work into visible external impact in 2025.

Global security discovery

MadeYouReset

Working with Imperva, researchers from the center uncovered a protocol-level flaw in HTTP/2 affecting more than 100 vendors and potentially billions of users.

Why it matters: we coordinated responsible disclosure through US CERT before the issue could be widely exploited, turning academic discovery into direct public protection.
